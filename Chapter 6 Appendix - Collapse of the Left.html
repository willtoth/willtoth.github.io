Appendix: The Collapse of the Left

The Great World War II proved to be a crisis for the political left. Soviet despotism gave anticommunism respectability, whereas credibility was lost for the left wing. Although what passed for socialism by the standards of Stalin thrived in Russia, the Marxist dream outside of Russia had no prospect of fruition when the war came along. Even in Soviet territory, leftists faced tribulations.

The left rapidly lost ground as anticommunism became the dominant force in Western politics. The right wing in turn took up the "counter-ideology" as its primary platform for popular support. With Soviet expansion fueling a desperate form of popularity, the right was able to bring the general public into its arms. Rightists, with the confidence of the masses, were thus winning elections, staging coups, and enforcing their policies with ease.

Not just was the left forced out of the branches of government, they were banned from the public square as well. The administrations of the right were quick to pass regulations outlawing the existence of Communist, Socialist, Social Democratic, and other leftist parties. A few parties of the left (and more to the mainstream) were the exceptions, being subject to checks that were otherwise mild compared to a full crackdown. As for workers' unions, they were only allowed to exist after having gone through a stringent process of registration and were granted licenses to legally operate. But these were only privileges granted by the more moderate right-wing regimes; the far-right governments of Francisco Franco and Benito Mussolini made no pretense about eradicating the existence of the left and organized labor within their borders. The remaining remnants of the more ideological left went underground, advancing their views while trying not to get caught. But being a fugitive-activist while avoiding the law was quite difficult, since demonstrations and rallies orchestrated by leftists and antiwar activists were strictly forbidden. Indeed, public statements made against the Allied war effort were viewed as suspect.

The Western left also saw a rift in its own ranks. The left-wing camp broke into two smaller camps, with one for the Soviets and the other against. The pro-Soviet faction of the left viewed the USSR as a vital step towards the realization of Marxism and its advance across the Continent as the harbinger for further revolutions of the proletariat. The anti-Soviet left, on the other hand, denounced Stalinism as a betrayal of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. Stances toward the war proved to be another divisive question. Some on the left, whether pro- or anti- Soviet,, called for conciliation between the Allied and Soviet sides. Others on the anti-Soviet left voiced their support for the Allied powers, whereas the pro-Soviet leftists condemned the Allied war effort (while at the same time abetting the Soviet advance). Nonetheless, the conflict of opinions within the left wing did not matter to the government and military authorities, who restricted the left and its advocates whenever they had the opportunity.

The left of Eastern Europe (which the Soviets claimed early on) also suffered its share of repression. Trotskyite and other anti-Stalin leftists faced either the gulag or the firing squad when Soviet forces moved in. Surviving anti-Soviet leftists took to guerilla warfare in their resistance of Soviet occupation. As for the Eastern European Communists who were devoted to Stalin, they held key positions in the puppet governments established by the Soviets and reported directly to Moscow.
